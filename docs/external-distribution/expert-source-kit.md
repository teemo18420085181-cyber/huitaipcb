### Q-type 1: "How do you vet an overseas / China contract manufacturer?"
> The single best test is simple: send them your Gerber files and BOM and watch what comes back.
> A supplier that fires back an instant price without looking at your design is quoting a fantasy —
> they'll hit problems in production and the 'cheap' quote balloons with change requests. The ones
> worth trusting come back with questions: a footprint that looks off, a part that's end-of-life, a
> tolerance that'll hurt yield. Manufacturability questions before a quote are a green flag, not a
> delay. Also insist on a defined inspection and test plan in writing — "we'll check it" is not a plan.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)

### Q-type 2: "Biggest mistakes startups make on their first PCB / PCBA order?"
> Three recurring ones. First, an incomplete BOM — no manufacturer part numbers, no approved
> alternates — which forces the factory to guess or stall. Second, choosing purely on the lowest
> quote; the cheapest number usually means corners on inspection or testing you'll pay for later.
> Third, sending no test criteria — if you don't define pass/fail, you can't be surprised when a
> board that "powers on" still fails in the field. Spend the extra hour on a clean BOM and a one-page
> test spec; it saves weeks.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)

### Q-type 3: "How should hardware teams handle component shortages / sourcing risk?"
> Build flexibility into the BOM before you need it. For every critical part, list one or two
> approved alternates and explicitly authorize your manufacturer to propose substitutes for jellybean
> components — resistors, caps, common regulators. Single-sourcing a $0.02 part is how a whole build
> gets held hostage. And share your real timeline: a CM who knows your production window can buy
> ahead on long-lead parts instead of discovering the problem at kitting.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)

### Q-type 4: "What actually drives PCB assembly cost?"
> It's rarely just board size. The real cost drivers are part count and placement complexity —
> fine-pitch ICs, BGAs, and double-sided assembly all add process steps — plus your test scope,
> panelization, and the minimum order quantities on the components themselves. Two boards the same
> size can differ 3x in assembly cost. That's also why instant online quotes are often wrong: they
> price the board, not your actual design.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)

### Q-type 5: "How do you move from prototype to volume production?"
> Don't assume your prototype price or process scales — re-quote at volume, because part pricing,
> panelization, and test approach all change. Lock your design revision and BOM before scaling;
> a running change mid-ramp is where defects sneak in. Run a proper DFM review and a small pilot
> run first to catch yield issues while they're cheap. And plan around component lead times early —
> at volume, a 16-week part can gate your whole launch.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)

### Q-type 6: "Tips for sourcing electronics manufacturing in China specifically?"
> Communication discipline matters more than price. Put everything in writing — files, quantities,
> test criteria, packaging, labeling — and confirm the factory repeats it back correctly. Use a
> supplier that reviews your design before quoting and replies fast; responsiveness on small things
> predicts responsiveness when something goes wrong. Start with a paid prototype or pilot batch
> before a big order; it tests the relationship as much as the boards.
> — Hu Maofu, General Manager, Huitai Electronics (huitaipcb.com)
