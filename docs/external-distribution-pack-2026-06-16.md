### 1. What Files Do You Need for a PCB Assembly Quote?

Target keyword: `what files are needed for PCB assembly`

Summary draft:

Many PCB assembly quote delays do not come from soldering or production. They start much earlier, when the supplier receives incomplete project files. For a practical PCBA quote, a buyer should normally prepare Gerber files, BOM, pick-and-place or centroid data, quantity, assembly side information, drawings if available, and any testing or packaging expectations.

Gerber files describe the PCB fabrication data. The BOM tells the supplier which components are needed. Pick-and-place data helps locate components on the board. When these files are incomplete, the supplier can still review the project, but the quotation may require extra engineering questions.

For turnkey PCBA, the review should also include BOM completeness, missing MPNs, out-of-stock parts, approved alternatives, PCB complexity, assembly process, and testing requirements. A good quote process is not just a price request; it is a production-readiness check.

### 2. BOM Sourcing Risks That Can Delay PCB Assembly

Target keyword: `BOM sourcing PCB assembly`

Summary draft:

BOM sourcing is often the hidden risk in PCB assembly. A project can have complete Gerber files and a clear quantity, but still be blocked by missing part numbers, obsolete components, long lead-time parts, unclear package information, or unavailable approved alternatives.

Before production, the BOM should be checked for MPN completeness, package matching, lifecycle risk, shortage risk, and whether any substitutions require customer approval. For overseas buyers, this review is especially useful because it can reveal sourcing problems before the assembly quote becomes a production order.

### 3. Prototype vs Low-Volume PCBA: What Should Buyers Prepare?

Target keyword: `prototype PCB assembly China`

Summary draft:

Prototype PCBA and low-volume PCBA are closely related, but they are not the same buying situation. A prototype build is usually about checking whether the design, BOM, assembly process, and basic testing plan are ready for a first build. Low-volume assembly is more about repeating the build in small quantities while controlling sourcing risk, consistency, and documentation.

For a prototype quote, buyers should prepare Gerber files, BOM, pick-and-place data if available, drawings, quantity, and any project notes. If files are incomplete, sample photos or old documentation may still help the supplier understand the scope. For low-volume builds such as 5, 10, 50, 100, or 500 pcs, quantity and component availability become especially important.

The best next step is an engineering review before quotation. This helps confirm whether the project can move from files to a practical PCBA build.

### Q3. What should I do if some BOM parts are out of stock?

First, do not approve substitutions blindly. Ask the assembler or sourcing partner to mark which parts are unavailable, obsolete, or long lead-time. Then review whether approved alternatives exist and whether they match package, key electrical parameters, lifecycle, and product requirements.

For PCB assembly, BOM shortage handling should happen before production, not after parts are already purchased. The customer or design owner should approve alternatives before they are used.

Light CTA: Huitai Electronics can review BOM shortage risks before PCB assembly quotation and discuss alternatives project by project.

### Q4. Can I order only 5 or 10 assembled PCBs?

Yes, some PCBA suppliers support very small prototype or low-volume builds. But the quote depends on more than quantity. The supplier will need to review Gerber files, BOM, component availability, assembly side, process complexity, and testing requirements.

For 5 or 10 pcs, the unit cost is usually higher than mass production because setup, sourcing, stencil, programming, and inspection work are spread across fewer boards.
